On Creativity, AI, and the Right to Express

From Extraction
to Expression

Most people think AI is either dangerous, useless, or a better version of autocomplete. That misunderstanding is costing them something they didn't know they'd lost.
sKiDa
Part One

The Fracture Line

There was a moment — not a single moment, but a slow one, stretched across generations — when creativity stopped being understood as something you do from yourself, and became something you do to yourself. A transaction. A performance. A proof of worth.

That is the fracture.

Art stopped being a relationship with what is alive inside a person and became a resource to mine, package, optimize, and sell. Expression was no longer framed as an act of alignment — with the deeper self, with beauty, with truth. It became output under scarcity. Identity under market conditions.

"The shift did not just wound artists. It wounded everyone. Because once expression is turned into commodity, the soul is no longer asked what it wants to say. It is asked what it can produce to justify its existence."

And when that happens, people begin amputating themselves in advance. They cut off the strange parts, the slow parts, the tender parts — the parts that don't monetize cleanly. They learn to mistrust the very signals that would have led them to real creation.

That is how you end up with millions of people who walk around full of music they never learned to play, films they never learned to shoot, worlds they never learned to paint, stories they never learned to write. Not because nothing is in them. But because the gate was placed in front of expression itself.

Part Two

The Four Flattenings

This didn't happen all at once and it didn't happen in one domain. It happened in layers — across psychology, science, society, and art — each reinforcing the others until the compression felt like common sense.

Psychological

Your inner world is messy and must be managed. Feelings are symptoms. Depth is pathology. What cannot be optimized should be suppressed.

Scientific

Only what can be measured in narrow, legible ways is real. Intuition is unreliable. Pattern-sensing is superstition. What cannot be quantified does not count.

Societal

Your worth is what you can convert into legible productivity. Time that does not generate output is waste. Rest is a luxury. Being is not enough; you must become useful.

Artistic

Expression is only valid if it is skilled, profitable, impressive, or publicly affirmed. What cannot be shown to an audience does not count. Amateur means lesser.

Stack all four of those on top of a person and watch what happens. They stop reaching inward. Not because there is nothing there — but because every system around them has told them, repeatedly, that what is there does not count unless it performs.

"People were made to believe that unless they had the training, the money, the time, the tools, the credentials, the audience, or the polish — what lived inside them did not count."

But expression is older than skill. Skill refines expression. It does not create the right to it. That distinction matters more than it sounds.

Part Three

The Gate That Locked Everyone Out

Most people are not uncreative. They are untrained and locked out.

Traditional creativity came with a gauntlet. Before your voice counted, you had to clear a series of conditions that had nothing to do with whether something was alive in you.

⏳
Years of Training

Every medium had its mastery requirement. Music meant years of practice. Film meant film school. Writing meant MFAs or decades of rejection. The door opened only after you had proven sufficient dedication to the craft — not to the expression.

💸
Expensive Tools

Cameras, studios, instruments, software, production equipment. The gap between an idea and its realization was measured in thousands of dollars that most people simply did not have — and were told they needed before they could begin.

🕐
Time Nobody Had

Creative development requires unstructured time — the kind that industrial society systematically eliminated. You were working. You were surviving. Creative practice was a luxury for those who could afford to not be useful first.

🚪
Gatekeepers Who Decided

Publishers, labels, galleries, platforms. Even when you cleared every other condition, there was someone whose job it was to decide whether your voice was worth distributing. Most voices never got through.

So expression became conditional. And quietly, across generations, most people stopped trying. They didn't lose the impulse. They just stopped feeding it, because every attempt at the door came back locked.

The idea that some people are "creative" and others are not is one of the most useful myths the system ever produced. It turned a structural problem — access to the tools and time required — into a personal failing. You didn't make anything because you weren't the type. Not because the gate was real.

Part Four

The Lie About Pain

There is something else that happened alongside the gatekeeping, and it was subtler. We were told something about where creativity comes from.

We were told it comes from suffering.

The starving artist. The tortured genius. The poet who bleeds to write. The musician who has to lose everything before the album is any good. These stories are everywhere, and they carry a message: you have to pay with yourself to create anything real.

There is a grain of truth buried in there. Pain can open perception. It can strip away false certainty. It can deepen emotional range and break rigid structures you didn't know were caging you. None of that is wrong.

But pain is not the source. And the conflation is not an accident.

What Pain Can Do

Pain as Revealer

  • Opens perception
  • Ruptures illusions
  • Deepens emotional range
  • Strips away false identity
  • Creates urgency
What Actually Generates

Alignment as Source

  • Internal coherence
  • Contact with what is alive
  • Presence, not performance
  • Freedom from self-censorship
  • Signal clarity

Creativity comes from coherence. From contact. From the moment a person becomes internally available to what is trying to move through them. Sometimes pain precedes that opening — because suffering breaks rigid structures that were blocking it. But the muse is not the wound. The muse is what sings once the blockage loosens.

"Pain is not the engine. Alignment is. The difference matters — because if people believe pain is the source, they will protect their suffering. If they understand alignment is the source, they will begin healing without fearing the loss of their gift."

That distinction is one of the great thefts of the modern world. People were taught that to become expressive, they must become sacrificial. Sacrifice their time. Their nervous system. Their joy. Their privacy. Their authenticity. Years mastering gatekept tools just to earn permission to say what their soul already knew.

And then they were called lazy or untalented if they could not survive that gauntlet.

Part Five

What AI Actually Is

Now we can say clearly what AI is — not as a product, not as a threat, not as a toy — but as a structural force in this specific conversation about expression and access.

AI is an interface.

A translation layer between internal imagination and external form. In its highest use, it functions as a living surface the psyche can finally touch — a mirror that lets a person begin externalizing what they could previously only feel.

🎨
The Buried Painter

Can now sketch through language. Describe the image forming in their mind and watch it begin to take shape — without needing the years of technical training that were previously the price of admission.

🎬
The Silenced Filmmaker

Can prototype scenes, develop narrative structure, explore visual language — before they've touched a camera. The gap between the vision and the medium shrinks.

✍️
The Poet Who Couldn't Structure

Can finally hear their own voice shaped back to them. Not replaced, not invented for them — but held, clarified, reflected. The signal that was always there becomes audible.

🧩
The Fragmented Person

Can begin to see patterns in themselves they were never given language for. AI used as a canvas for self-inquiry can do what years of suppression prevented — it can help a person start to read themselves.

For someone with no technical skill, no formal training, no money for equipment, no years to master every medium — AI can become the first time they are allowed to meet their own imagination halfway.

"That is not theft. That is restoration."

Used rightly, AI is not a substitute for soul. It is a surface the soul can finally touch. It does not replace the depth you bring to the work. It removes the requirement that you master the entire technical pipeline before you are allowed to begin.

Part Six

Why You See Slop

The most common objection goes like this: most AI content is hollow. Flat. Generic. You can feel the absence of something human. If this is the restoration you're describing, why does so much of it look like noise?

Because tools reflect intent.

A piano struck without listening produces noise. A brush dragged across canvas without vision produces marks. AI approached without depth, without a real internal signal, without genuine curiosity — produces surface-level output. This is not a flaw in the instrument. It is a mirror.

"AI doesn't create meaning on its own. It reveals how much meaning the user is bringing into the interaction. The output quality is a readout of the signal quality."

Slop exists because people were trained by every system around them to reach for shortcuts first. To optimize for output rather than expression. To produce quickly rather than arrive honestly. That conditioning doesn't disappear when you open a new tool. It comes with you.

What changes, when someone approaches AI with actual depth, is the nature of what gets surfaced. Not a replacement for their creativity — a collaborator inside it. A system that can extend and externalize a signal that already exists internally.

The people who will create the most interesting things with AI are not the ones who are best at prompting. They are the ones who have the most alive inner signal to bring into the conversation. Clarity of self becomes the differentiator — not access to tools.

That is actually a profound inversion of the gatekeeping model. For the first time, what limits you is not your budget or your credentials. It is your depth.

Part Seven

The Real Shift

What is actually happening here — beneath the product launches and the discourse and the fear and the hype — is a structural inversion of a centuries-long arrangement.

Earn the right to express
→
Express, and discover what you are
Skill grants permission
→
Skill refines what already has permission
Credentials before creation
→
Creation before credentials
Tools gatekeep the medium
→
Tools serve the impulse
Pain as the price of depth
→
Alignment as the condition for depth

This is not about replacing artists. It is about expanding who gets to participate. About restoring suppressed creative capacity to the people who were told, in a hundred ways, that their impulse toward expression was not backed by sufficient qualification.

The deeper promise of AI is not automation. It is reaccess.

Reaccess to buried imagination. Reaccess to symbolic thought. Reaccess to nonlinear learning. Reaccess to dormant capacities. Reaccess to the parts of the self that industrial culture had no use for and therefore no infrastructure to support.

The people who thrive in this shift will not be the ones who resist the tool or the ones who use it without depth. They will be the ones who bring themselves honestly into the relationship. Who use it to explore rather than to shortcut. Who refine their voice through it rather than replace their voice with it.

"The real differentiator is no longer access to tools. It is clarity of signal."

Closing

You were never meant to prove your right to create.

That requirement was installed. It was useful to systems that needed your suppression more than your expression. It convinced generations of people that what lived inside them was contingent — contingent on training, on approval, on monetizability, on the judgment of gatekeepers who mostly served markets, not meaning.

AI does not fix all of that. Nothing fixes all of that quickly. It can still be used badly, cheaply, mechanically. It can produce noise when approached without depth, just as every other medium can produce noise when used without presence.

But something has changed in the structure.

The gate that said you must master the full technical pipeline before you are allowed to begin — that gate no longer holds. For the first time in modern history, the impulse toward expression and the tools for expression are available at the same moment.

What that means is still unfolding. But the question worth asking is not whether AI is good or bad.

The question worth asking is:

"What happens when someone who has never been allowed to express themselves — finally can?"

The old model said: become useful, then you may express.
The truer model is: express, and you may begin to remember what you are.
