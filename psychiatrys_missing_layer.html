The Field · Essay Eight

Psychiatry's Missing Layer

The patterns are real. The theory is incomplete. This essay is not an attack on psychiatry. It is a completion of it — the layer the field cannot see by design, and what happens to people who fall through the gap.

sKiDa
The Field · Essay Eight
Part One

Begin With What Is True

The patterns are real.

People do oscillate between states that wreck their sleep, their relationships, their sense of self. People do dissociate. People do hear what others cannot, see what others do not, move through time in ways that other minds find unrecognizable. Some of these states resolve. Some of them compound. Some of them, untreated, kill the person carrying them. Suicide is not a metaphor. The damage from acute psychiatric crisis is not a social construction.

This essay does not deny the patterns. It does not deny that medication has, in specific cases, been the difference between someone surviving the week and not. It does not deny that stabilization is sometimes the precondition for any deeper work to even become possible. The mainstream is not wrong about needing intervention.

It is incomplete about what intervention is for.

The patterns are real. The theory of where they came from is not.

That is the whole essay, in one line. Everything that follows is the slow work of unpacking what it means.

Part Two

The Field Operating Without a Theory of Cause

Psychiatry, as currently practiced, has no working theory of why most of its patterns exist. This is not a slur. It is the field's own admission, made quietly, in journals that the public does not read.

The chemical imbalance theory of depression — the one most patients were told for thirty years — was never supported by the evidence claimed for it, and the field has now formally backed away from it. Nothing has replaced it. Antidepressants still work for some people, but no one knows why, including the prescribers. The DSM is not a theory of mental illness; it is a phenomenology of symptoms organized by clinical consensus and revised by committee. Its diagnoses do not map onto identifiable biological mechanisms. They map onto recognizable clusters of suffering. There is a difference, and the difference is the entire problem.

Meanwhile, the field cannot tell you, in any rigorous sense, how the brain produces consciousness. It cannot tell you where memories are stored. It cannot tell you what a thought is, mechanistically. It cannot point to the neurological substrate of a single major psychiatric diagnosis with the kind of confidence that would justify the certainty of the diagnostic act. The brain remains, in 2026, largely a black box. The field treating disorders of that black box is operating without a working manual.

None of this is conspiracy. It is the field's own literature. Read Joanna Moncrieff. Read Robert Whitaker. Read the British Psychological Society's Power Threat Meaning Framework, which explicitly reframes diagnosis as a political act. Read what the critical psychiatry movement has been saying from inside the profession for forty years.

And while we are being precise: this is also the field that, within living memory, won a Nobel Prize for the lobotomy. That treated homosexuality as illness until 1973. That diagnosed enslaved people who fled captivity with drapetomania. That gave us insulin coma therapy, recovered memory hysteria, the chemical castration of women called difficult, the warehousing of the inconvenient. The history is not a few embarrassing footnotes. The history is the field. The corrections, when they came, came late, and the people harmed in the meantime did not get their lives back.

None of this means current treatment is worthless. It means current treatment is operating on a theory of cause that the field itself cannot defend. Patients are not told this. They are told the opposite.

You cannot complete a system from inside its own frame. The missing layer is not a refinement of the existing theory. It is the theory the existing one was built without.

Part Three

The Misclassification Wound, at Scale

An earlier essay in this series traced a pattern in the culture: high-charge internal states, when they have no symbolic container, get collapsed into the nearest available category. Most often: sex. The body lights up; the culture supplies one word for it; the rest of the language goes dark.

What that essay described at the cultural level happens at the institutional level too. Same mechanism. Different translator.

When a person walks into a clinical setting with intensity moving through them — grief that exceeds the cultural permission window, activation from old trauma, sensory overload from a nervous system the world was not designed for, the early stages of what older traditions called spiritual emergence, the symbolic processing that happens when consciousness encounters material it cannot yet integrate — the clinical translator has, at most, a few hundred categories to route them into. The DSM is finite. The categories are flat. They were built for symptom recognition, not for source.

So the translation happens.

What May Actually Be Moving What Gets Recorded in the Chart
Spiritual emergence → Psychotic episode
Trauma activation in a high-charge nervous system → Bipolar disorder
Complex relational injury → Personality disorder
Grief that exceeds permission → Major depressive disorder
Symbolic processing in a non-Western frame → Delusional thinking
High sensitivity in a flat environment → Generalized anxiety disorder
Suppressed creative or vocational signal → Persistent depressive disorder

The arrows are not equivalence. They are routing. The thing on the left is what the system may actually be carrying. The thing on the right is what gets written down, billed, medicated, and entered into the lifelong record. The translation is not malicious. The translator is not a villain. Most clinicians would prefer to do better and have not been given the tools.

But the wound is the same as the cultural one, scaled up. The system is not broken because the energy is wrong. The system is broken because the translation layer is wrong.

Part Four

A Case Study Worth the Whole Essay

Of all the diagnoses where the translation problem is clearest, bipolar disorder may be the sharpest case. It deserves its own treatment. Not because the suffering it names is fictional — it is not — but because the gap between what the system sees and what the person is actually carrying is, in this diagnosis, almost the entire story.

What the Clinic Sees

The clinic sees oscillation. Periods of elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, increased energy, expansive ideation, sometimes grandiosity, sometimes risk-taking, sometimes psychotic features. Followed by, or alternating with, periods of profound depression — anhedonia, fatigue, suicidality, collapse. The pattern is recognizable. It is real. It can be deadly. The diagnostic criteria capture something. The medications, in many cases, dampen the oscillation enough for the person to function.

What the clinic does not see — because nothing in its training points it there — is what the oscillation may actually be doing.

What the Oscillation May Actually Be

A nervous system carrying more charge than it has containers for will not sit still. It cannot. The charge has to go somewhere, and if no integration channel is available, the system will improvise. It will swing. Up into expansion when the pressure becomes unbearable. Down into collapse when the expansion produces consequences the system cannot metabolize, or when the surrounding world refuses to receive what was rising. Then up again. Then down again. The oscillation is not random. It is a regulation strategy in a system that has too much signal and nowhere to put it.

What is the signal made of? Some of it is trauma — old material that was never discharged, returning under pressure. Some of it is unprocessed grief, sometimes ancestral, sometimes personal. Some of it is what older traditions across nearly every culture would have recognized without hesitation as spiritual emergence — the nervous system encountering material that is genuinely larger than the personal self, with no framework to receive it. Some of it is the high-sensitivity wiring of a person who was born to register more than the average environment is prepared to acknowledge.

In the absence of containers — symbolic, communal, ritual, developmental — the system does what it can. It runs the charge through the only channels available. When the available channels are inadequate, it overloads. When it overloads, it breaks symmetry. It swings.

Why Suppression Makes It Worse

Here is where the cultural and clinical mechanisms converge. A person who, from childhood, has been told that their intensity is too much, their sensitivities are imagined, their visions are not real, their grief is excessive, their joy is inappropriate, their needs are extreme — that person learns to suppress. The signal does not disappear. It cannot. The first law of psychic systems is the same as the first law of thermodynamics: energy is conserved. Suppressed signal pressurizes. Pressurized signal seeks any available release. When release happens through the only channels the culture has left open — sexual, manic, dissociative, compulsive — the release is read by the system as confirmation that something is wrong with the person, which produces more shame, which produces more suppression, which produces more pressure.

This is not metaphor. This is feedback dynamics. A high-charge nervous system in a low-container environment will oscillate. The oscillation will look exactly like what the DSM calls bipolar disorder, because phenomenologically, in many cases, that is what bipolar disorder is. Not in every case. Not as a universal claim. But in a significant fraction of cases — and the fraction is larger than the field is currently willing to admit — what is being treated as a chronic neurochemical condition is the late-stage signature of a translation failure that began in childhood.

What the Treatment Does, and Does Not Do

Mood stabilizers and antipsychotics, when they work, work by reducing the amplitude of the oscillation. They do not address the charge. They suppress the system's capacity to express it. For some people, this is a life-saving trade — the oscillation was going to kill them, and the muted version is survivable. For others, it is a catastrophic flattening. The signal that was trying to move gets pinned. The person becomes more functional and less alive. The diagnosis becomes lifelong because the underlying charge was never addressed; only its expression was muted. When the medication is reduced, the oscillation returns, which the field interprets as proof that the medication was necessary, when what it actually proves is that the source was never touched.

Some people do better off medication, with extensive somatic and symbolic work. Some people do better on medication for life. Some people do better on medication for a season and then, once integration work has reduced the charge to something the system can carry, are able to come off it under careful supervision. The field has no framework for distinguishing between these three populations because it does not currently believe the third population exists. It does. There are thousands of testimonies. The professional literature does not yet include them, but the literature follows the bodies, and the bodies are speaking.

What Was Suppressed

If you ask the bipolar person — when they are stable, neither manic nor depressed, just present — what they were carrying before they were diagnosed, you will often hear some version of: I felt too much. I saw things others did not see. I knew things I was not supposed to know. I was told I was wrong about all of it. I tried to make it stop. It would not stop.

That is not delusion. That is testimony. It is what suppression of a high-signal system sounds like in plain language. The diagnosis names the pattern of breakdown. It does not name what was being broken. What was being broken was, in many cases, a person whose nervous system was built to register sacred and complex signal that the surrounding world had no language for. The breakdown is real. The patterns are real. And what was being suppressed was real, too. All three things are simultaneously true.

Part Five

Pattern and Source

This is where the essay has to be careful, because the next move is the one that makes the whole framework either useful or dangerous.

Naming the source does not erase the pattern.

Once a nervous system has spent twenty years routing high charge through inadequate channels, the routing itself becomes the problem. The grooves are cut. The neural pathways are now habit. The endocrine cascade has its own momentum. The relational damage is real damage. The lost decade is a lost decade. You cannot rename your way out of any of it. You cannot affirm a person back to health when their system has been operating in dysregulation for so long that dysregulation is now its baseline.

This is why treatment matters. This is why the mainstream is not wrong about intervention. The damage compounds, and at some point the damage becomes its own thing — its own pattern — that requires its own work, regardless of whether the original cause is correctly named.

Layer One

The Pattern

The dysregulation, the grooves, the compounded damage, the neurochemical and relational momentum that now operates on its own. Real. Treatable. Sometimes requires medication, stabilization, structured intervention. The field is correct that this layer exists and correct that it must be addressed.

Layer Two

The Source

The misclassification. The unprocessed charge. The signal the culture had no container for. The trauma that was never discharged because there was nowhere to put it. The sacred or symbolic material that was diagnosed as illness because the diagnostic system has no other category. The field cannot see this layer because nothing in its training points it there.

Treatment that addresses only Layer One holds the symptom and never resolves the cause. The person stays on medication for life because the source keeps generating new pressure that the medication keeps muting. Treatment that addresses only Layer Two — the well-meaning spiritual or somatic practitioner who tells a person in active mania that they are awakening — abandons the person in acute danger to a framework that cannot stabilize them.

Both layers, held together, is the work. The field has the first half. The work of this lineage is to provide the second.

Mainstream psychiatry treats the damage. It does not treat the cause. It does not treat the cause because it cannot see the cause. Both halves of that sentence have to be held at once.

The patterns are real.
The damage is real.
And the source the field cannot see
is real too.
Part Six

What Completion Looks Like

Completion is not replacement. It is layering.

It looks like clinicians who can hold both that this client may need lithium and that this client is also carrying unprocessed material from a six-generation lineage of grief that no one in the family was permitted to feel. It looks like prescribing patterns that ask not only what is the diagnostic code but what charge is this person carrying that has not been allowed to move. It looks like treatment plans that include the medication and the somatic work and the symbolic work and the communal work, because all four address different layers and none of them substitutes for the others. It looks like a profession honest enough to tell its patients: we know how to dampen the oscillation. We do not yet know what the oscillation was trying to do. The work of finding out is yours, and we will support it instead of pathologizing it.

It also looks like an apology owed to a great many people. People who were told their sensitivity was a disorder. People who were told their grief was depression. People who were told their visions were psychosis. People who were told their activation was personality pathology. People who were medicated for forty years for what was, at the source, a translation problem the field could not see. Most of them are still alive. Some of them are not. The ones who are not are owed the same reckoning that the ones who are still here are owed. The field does not currently know how to give it. That does not relieve it of the debt.

The Completion Question

For anyone — clinician, client, family member, person reading this who has carried a diagnosis for a decade and never quite believed it explained them — the question that begins the completion is the same one the previous essay in this series ended with, scaled up:

The Question

If this pattern had a source, and the source was not chemical, what was it?

Not as a replacement for the diagnostic answer. As an addition to it. The person almost always knows. They have just never been given permission to look.

The diagnosis is real. The treatment is sometimes necessary. The pattern is doing its damage. And underneath all of it, there is almost always something the system was trying to carry that no one taught it how to hold. Finding out what — slowly, with support, without abandoning the stabilization that is keeping the person alive — is the work the field has not yet learned to do.

Until it learns, this lineage will keep doing it on the outside. We are not attacking psychiatry. We are completing it. The completion is owed.
